Experience Flow

The V!Real(ity) experience guides participants through five distinct stages, each designed to challenge and expand understanding of visual perception and reality.

01

RGB Painting & DMX Lights

Visual Transformation

Setup: Experience begins in complete darkness with narration introducing the concept that perception requires casting a light.

Interaction: RGB lights cycle through red, blue, green, yellow, and white, revealing completely different images on the same wall under each colored filter.

Outcome: Participants observe how different perspectives are unlocked through each colored light, demonstrating that the same physical space can reveal multiple realities.

02

Color Detection

Perception Challenge

Transition: Narration explains the previous RGB experience, followed by a Continue button.

Challenge: Users detect the color box that differs from the rest. Difficulty progresses from easier to harder levels, testing ability to distinguish subtle color variations.

Learning: Narration discusses how humans detect color differences, followed by another Continue button to proceed.

03

Color Matching

Spatial Perception

Task: Users attempt to match colors with a reference object.

Revelation: Spatial and angular aspects affect color perception—distance and viewing angle introduce perceptual biases that make accurate matching challenging.

Conclusion: Narration concludes the color matching experience, followed by a Continue button.

04

ASCII Face Perception

Predictive Processing

Visual Effect: An ASCII mask appears on the face of the person standing in front of the screen.

Concept: Demonstrates predictive processing—ASCII characters are random key blocks, but our brains interpret them as recognizable faces.

Insight: If someone walks in without having seen the person before, they cannot recognize the face until viewing it in real-time.

Transition: After 10 seconds, screen fades to black displaying "Please proceed to the VR experience."

05

VR Experience

Immersive Reality

Experience: Participants navigate through VR scenarios demonstrating how different perceptions lead to different choices.

Narrative: The guided experience follows a person making decisions based on their unique perception of each scene.

Thesis: Reinforces the core concept that perception shapes reality.

The Vision Thief Card Game

Overview

A discussion-based, socially deductive card game that facilitates conversations about alternative futures. The Vision Thief places players in perspectives they may never engage with, employing futurist methods of metaphor and foresight thinking to create collective narratives of a more hopeful future.

TYPE

Card Game, Speculative Design, Game Design, Futures Thinking

ROLE

Game Designer, Visual Designer

TOOL

Hand Sketching, Gemini AI, Figma

SKILL

Game Design, Speculative Design, Visual Design

PROJECT SNAPSHOT

Goal

Create a discussion-based, socially deductive card game that facilitates conversations about alternative futures. The game aims to promote different perspectives, place players in mindsets they may have never engaged with, build trust and community, while employing futurist concepts of metaphor and foresight thinking to create collective narratives of a more hopeful future.

Challenge

The initial design was too rigid with direct mechanical linkage between cards and points, causing player confusion. The challenge was shifting from a rigid, card-based reward system to a dynamic, socially deductive one. This required decoupling the point system from prompt cards entirely and transforming simple single-word topics into rich, conversational prompts that inspire meaningful narrative contributions.

Outcome

Successfully created a card game that fosters emergent narrative and collaborative storytelling. By decoupling scoring from prompt cards, the game transformed into a powerful framework for meaningful conversations. Players became more engaged with the narrative-driven approach, and the game now serves as a microcosm of real-world social challenges, encouraging critical engagement with systemic obstacles to progress.

Reconnect Booth

Overview

A speculative design project exploring how to sustain human connection in a hyper-efficient, digitally-dependent future. The Reconnect Booth imagines Abu Dhabi in 2045, where automation and efficiency have led to "quiet disconnection." The project proposes a physical booth where two strangers or acquaintances engage in brief, guided, offline interactions—acting as a "relational speed bump" to repair the social fabric.

TYPE

Speculative Design, Futures Thinking, Interactive Installation

ROLE

Researcher, Prototype Designer

TOOL

API-Integration, Fire Database, Html+Java, Figma

SKILL

Ethnography, Prototyping, Urban Design

PROJECT SNAPSHOT

Goal

Create a speculative public infrastructure that addresses "quiet disconnection" in a hyper-efficient future. The Reconnect Booth aims to act as a "relational speed bump," forcing people to pause their high-speed digital lives and engage in meaningful, face-to-face reflection. The project explores what if a city's infrastructure was designed specifically to repair the social fabric rather than just move people or data.

Challenge

Designing for a future where digital convenience has eroded slow, in-person rituals, creating loneliness as a major public health concern. The challenge was to create a believable future scenario using structured foresight methods, then prototype and test how humans actually behave when "forced" to connect. Cultural sensitivity was crucial—the booths must respect privacy and autonomy while leaning into Gulf values of community and hospitality.

Outcome

Successfully created a physical prototype that demonstrated people prefer "guided" social moments in a fast-paced world because it reduces the effort of starting conversations. The structured nature of the booth made connecting easier by removing social pressure. The project proved that future urban planning should treat "connection" as a utility—just like water or electricity—and that emotional infrastructure can be fully integrated into urban planning.

Research & Frameworks

Strategic Scanning (STEEP-V)

Identified weak signals: Micro-Social apps, wellness pods in airports, AI as social icebreaker. Mapped drivers across Social (fragmented family time), Environmental (extreme heat), and Political (social cohesion as policy metric) dimensions.

2×2 Scenario Framework

Mapped uncertainties: Technological Mediation vs. Civic Involvement. Chose scenario: High Tech / High Civic Support—a world where emotional well-being is a public right, with government-provided "Wellness Spots" and "Reconnect Booths" as free public services.

Three Horizons & Futures Triangle

Horizon 1: Digital convenience eroding slow, in-person rituals. Horizon 2: Digital fatigue sets in; people crave grounding experiences. Horizon 3: Emotional infrastructure integrated into urban planning. The Triangle balances Pull (desire for well-being), Push (automation/loneliness), and Weight (traditional Gulf values of hospitality).

Anticipatory Ethnography

Built a physical prototype to test human behavior when "forced" to connect. Conducted diary studies with participants recording feelings over time. Key finding: Initially awkward and hesitant, but structured nature made connecting easier by removing social pressure of knowing what to say.

Enterprise Overview and Market Context

This paper examines three organizations illustrating how impact investing operates across different models of ethical supply chain reform: a product-based social enterprise, a certification body, and a technology platform.

The global smartphone market ($457 billion in 2023) faces scrutiny over conflict minerals and e-waste, though ethical electronics remain under 1% of sales. Founded in the Netherlands in 2013, Fairphone produces modular smartphones (€499–€699) built for longevity and repairability with responsibly sourced materials. Having sold 400,000 units and earned €40.5 million in 2022, Fairphone aims not for scale but to prove that demand for ethical electronics exists, influencing larger manufacturers toward responsible practices.

In the textiles and carpet sector ($80 billion annually), child labor remains widespread in South Asian supply chains. Established in 1994 by Nobel laureate Kailash Satyarthi, GoodWeave certifies products made without child labor. Producers pay licensing fees (0.25–2% of export value) for the GoodWeave label, which commands 5–15% price premiums. In 2021, the organization generated $3.8 million in revenue—$877,000 from fees and $2.93 million from grants—while certifying over 180 facilities. However, smaller producers often struggle with compliance costs.

The supply chain compliance market ($8.2 billion in 2022) increasingly seeks worker-centered alternatives to traditional audits. Labor Voices, a for-profit social enterprise, enabled anonymous mobile reporting on factory conditions for $50,000–$200,000 annual corporate subscriptions. Despite working with major brands, it ceased operations in 2019 due to limited smartphone access, cultural barriers to reporting, and brand reluctance to fund transparency initiatives.

Business Models and Value Creation

Social enterprises balance social impact and profitability through varying operational logics. Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices exemplify three distinct approaches: direct alignment between sales and impact (lock-step), prioritization of mission over revenue (trade-off), and an attempted hybrid model that proved unsustainable.

Fairphone operates a lock-step model in which commercial success directly scales social impact. Each smartphone sale (€550 on average) generates both revenue and measurable ethical outcomes—expanded living-wage programs, increased recycled material use, and strengthened supplier partnerships. The company's entire revenue derives from product sales, linking financial growth to mission delivery. Yet Fairphone's 15–20% higher production costs, driven by ethical sourcing and smaller volumes, limit competitiveness.

GoodWeave follows a trade-off model, prioritizing mission sustainability over market independence. Producers pay certification fees (23% of revenue) while philanthropic funding (77%) supports inspections, education, and advocacy programs. The model reflects a deliberate acceptance of financial dependence on external donors—including the Skoll Foundation, UNICEF, and government partners—to maintain accessibility for smaller producers and uphold rigorous monitoring standards that market mechanisms alone cannot sustain.

Labor Voices adopted a subscription-based B2B model, providing real-time factory condition data to corporate clients for $50,000–$200,000 annually. The company's dual reliance on worker participation and brand adoption created structural fragility: limited smartphone access, cultural barriers to reporting, and brand hesitancy toward transparency constrained scalability. Despite backing from Humanity United and Omidyar Network, Labor Voices collapsed in 2019, underscoring how lock-step models can fail when market incentives and ethical imperatives diverge.

Impact Investing and Ethical Supply Chains

Lessons from Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices

Introduction

Global consumerism has created vast supply chains that, while delivering unprecedented access to goods, often obscure severe ethical and environmental abuses.

From cobalt mining for electronics to garment production for fast fashion, forced labor and unsafe working conditions remain pervasive. With an estimated 27.6 million people in forced labor and 79 million children in hazardous work, these violations directly undermine the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth) and SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production).

Yet these same crises have spurred a growing ethical market. The fair trade sector alone was valued at $11.3 billion in 2023, projected to grow 8.5% annually through 2030. Similarly, the sustainable electronics market is expanding under consumer and regulatory pressure around e-waste and conflict minerals. Within this context, impact investing—capital deployed to generate measurable social and environmental benefits alongside profit—has become a key driver of reform. Investments targeting ethical supply chains reached $12.4 billion in 2023, signaling growing investor belief in socially responsible business models.

This paper argues that impact investing can enable more ethical supply chains, but its success depends on three interlinked factors: (1) alignment between business models and social missions, (2) the rigor of quality control and monitoring systems, and (3) the motivations shaping investor strategies.

Investment Ecosystem and Capital Allocation

Understanding how these enterprises secure funding reveals distinct investor motivations and financial structures that shape their capacity for impact.

Fairphone has raised about €79 million since 2013, beginning with a €400,000 crowdfunding campaign that validated consumer demand, followed by impact-focused equity rounds with Triodos Bank, DOEN Participaties, and Invest-NL. These investors provide patient capital with 5–8% return expectations—well below market rates—to support ethical sourcing despite higher production costs. Fairphone's funding mix includes quasi-equity and long-term instruments from development finance institutions, reflecting investor willingness to trade profitability for systemic influence, such as normalizing ethical practices across the electronics sector.

GoodWeave operates on a philanthropic model, with cumulative funding exceeding $60 million since 1994 and annual budgets around $3–4 million. Major contributors—Skoll Foundation, UNICEF, U.S. Department of Labor, and corporate foundations—supply unrestricted and program-specific grants that sustain monitoring, education, and advocacy initiatives. These funders prioritize verified child welfare outcomes over financial return, viewing GoodWeave's certification and rescue programs as high-impact, replicable interventions. However, dependence on grants limits scalability and exposes the organization to shifts in donor priorities.

Labor Voices secured roughly $4–6 million in seed and Series A funding from Humanity United, Omidyar Network, and angel investors. Its venture-capital structure, based on convertible notes and equity, required commercial scalability—an expectation that clashed with market realities. Corporate hesitancy to fund transparency tools and low worker smartphone access hindered adoption, leading to the enterprise's closure in 2019.

Ultimately, capital structure determines impact viability: patient equity sustains Fairphone's ethical sourcing, grant funding enables GoodWeave's deep social outcomes, and venture capital constraints contributed to Labor Voices' collapse when mission and market incentives diverged.

Impact Measurement and Management

For social enterprises, Impact Measurement and Management (IMM) ensures accountability and investor confidence by linking mission-driven goals to verifiable outcomes. Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices employ distinct frameworks aligned with their operating models but share common challenges in attribution, consistency, and sustaining systemic change.

Fairphone applies a data-driven IMM framework aligned with the Global Reporting Initiative and B Corp standards. Its 2023 Impact Report records a 48% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions since 2022 and 69.5% fair or recycled materials in its Fairbuds line. Social indicators include $1 million in living-wage bonuses and programs benefiting around 20,000 workers. These quantifiable KPIs reassure investors of credible progress, though attribution remains difficult, as broader industry shifts may also influence results.

GoodWeave employs an audit-based IMM framework emphasizing direct human outcomes under SDG 8 and SDG 16. For example, its 2021 Annual Report documented that more than 101,100 workers were protected under its standard in that year alone. Independent audits verify outcomes, offering donors concrete welfare metrics. However, maintaining compliance post-certification remains challenging, as economic pressures can reintroduce exploitative practices.

Labor Voices measured digital engagement through survey participation, issue reporting, and resolution rates under the IRIS+ "worker empowerment" indicators. While providing scalable, data-rich metrics, inconsistent reporting due to language, literacy, and trust barriers undermined data reliability. Moreover, brands' limited response to worker feedback weakened both social impact and investor confidence.

Collectively, these enterprises show that IMM has evolved from a reporting mechanism into a strategic tool shaping capital allocation. Yet the effectiveness of such systems depends on translating measurement into verified, lasting change—a criterion essential to sustaining investor trust and long-term viability.

Quality Control, Risk Management, and Operational Challenges

Upholding ethical standards in global supply chains involves operational, financial, and reputational risks that shape investor confidence and enterprise viability. The quality control mechanisms used by Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices reveal both the potential and limits of ethical supply chain reform.

Fairphone faces operational challenges sourcing ethically from conflict regions and maintaining fair labor practices across dispersed suppliers. It relies on direct relationships and regular audits in the DRC and China but remains vulnerable to political instability and supplier constraints due to small production volumes. Ethical sourcing premiums (15–20%) strain profit margins, while unverifiable claims risk accusations of greenwashing. Transparency efforts—such as supply chain mapping and B Corp certification—mitigate some concerns, though lower-tier supplier opacity persists.

GoodWeave ensures compliance through unannounced inspections and audits, but risks arise when subcontracting extends beyond monitoring reach. Maintaining inspection infrastructure exceeds fee revenue, creating financial dependence on fluctuating donor funding. The greatest reputational threat is certification fraud, forcing a balance between strict enforcement and supportive remediation. Market risks include consolidation among retailers and exclusion of smaller producers unable to afford compliance, limiting the certification's reach.

Labor Voices relied on mobile technology to gather worker feedback, but limited smartphone access, fear of retaliation, and language barriers undermined data reliability. High development costs and client hesitancy to expose labor issues constrained revenue. The absence of physical audits to verify digital reports weakened credibility, eroding investor trust and leading to its closure.

Together, these cases highlight key trade-offs: Fairphone's control limits scalability; GoodWeave's scalability strains enforcement; Labor Voices' efficiency sacrifices verification. For impact investors, such operational realities inform risk assessments, showing that sustaining ethical standards depends not only on mission but on the practical capacity to uphold them amid commercial and logistical pressures.

Comparative Analysis: Navigating the Investment Trilemma

The investment trilemma posits that social enterprises face inherent tensions between deep impact, financial profitability, and scalability, rarely optimizing all three simultaneously.

Examining how Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices navigate these trade-offs reveals why certain combinations of business model, capital structure, and quality control mechanisms succeed while others fail.

Fairphone pursues balanced positioning, achieving moderate impact through verified ethical sourcing while maintaining financial viability through premium pricing, though sacrificing rapid scalability. Direct supplier relationships enable quality control but limit production volumes. Patient equity investors (Triodos Bank, Invest-NL) accepting 5-8% returns explicitly choose this trade-off, recognizing that demonstration effects on industry giants constitute success regardless of market share. Geographic concentration in Western Europe reflects market realities where consumers are willing to pay €499-€699 clustered in high-income regions with strong environmental consciousness. This investor-enterprise alignment around "proof of concept" rather than market dominance explains Fairphone's sustainability despite niche positioning.

GoodWeave maximizes impact depth at profitability's expense, operating a trade-off model where philanthropic capital subsidizes labor-intensive auditing that certification fees cannot sustain. Unannounced inspections provide quality control credibility essential to certification value, yet $2.93 million annual grant dependence creates financial vulnerability and limits geographic expansion to donor-prioritized regions. Philanthropic investors (Skoll Foundation, UNICEF) explicitly accept zero financial return for verified child welfare outcomes, with risk tolerance focused on reputational damage from certification fraud rather than profitability. However, this model excludes smaller producers unable to afford compliance costs, concentrating impact among larger manufacturers. Geographic and sector-specific challenges include varying regulatory enforcement across South Asian countries and cultural factors affecting audit effectiveness.

Labor Voices attempted simultaneous optimization through technology-enabled efficiency, but this lock-step model collapsed when quality control mechanisms proved inadequate and market demand failed to materialize. Venture capital investors expected 15-25% IRR alongside SDG 8 alignment, anticipating corporate accountability regulations would drive subscription growth. However, the platform's inability to verify self-reported worker data undermined both investor confidence and brand willingness to pay for untrustworthy insights. Unlike Fairphone's physical audits or GoodWeave's in-person inspections, Labor Voices lacked verification infrastructure, creating a fatal quality control gap. Geographic challenges including uneven smartphone penetration and cultural barriers to reporting amplified operational risks, while costs requiring simultaneous technology development and market education exceeded venture capital patience.

Successful navigation depends on aligning three elements: the enterprise's chosen trade-offs, investor risk tolerance and return expectations, and quality control mechanisms appropriate to the business model.

Fairphone succeeds because patient equity matches its moderate-impact positioning and direct supplier relationships provide credible verification. GoodWeave succeeds because philanthropic capital accepts its deep-impact, zero-profit reality and physical auditing justifies certification premiums. Labor Voices failed because venture capital's profitability requirements conflicted with inability to demonstrate scalable, verifiable impact through technology alone.

Impact investors must assess not merely business model elegance but practical capacity to maintain ethical standards through credible quality control mechanisms amid commercial pressures and resource constraints.

Conclusion

This analysis shows that impact investing can promote ethical supply chains, but success depends on aligning enterprise design, investor strategy, and quality control capacity. Fairphone, GoodWeave, and Labor Voices illustrate how outcomes hinge on the balance between impact, profitability, and scalability: patient equity sustains Fairphone's moderate-impact model, philanthropic grants support GoodWeave's deep-impact mission, while venture capital proved incompatible with Labor Voices' verification gaps.

Reliable quality control—through physical audits or certification—builds investor confidence, whereas unverified digital reporting erodes it. Each case also exposes trade-offs. Fairphone's reach remains limited to niche ethical consumers, questioning whether it drives systemic change. GoodWeave's certification may exclude small producers, concentrating benefits among larger manufacturers. Labor Voices' failure underscores how technology-driven transparency falters when corporate incentives resist exposure of labor issues.

Impact investing thus holds transformative potential but requires patience, realistic return expectations, and rigorous impact measurement. True systemic reform depends not just on capital, but on sustained alignment among investors, enterprises, and institutions committed to embedding ethics within global supply chains.

Problem Statement & User Research

Identified a significant campus pain point through comprehensive user research. Surveyed 164 students, revealing that 16.5% (27 students) had experienced stolen or lost clothes due to inefficient laundry monitoring systems. This data-driven approach informed the entire project scope and technical requirements.

01

Objectives & Scope

Reduce Overcrowding: Real-time monitoring of machine availability to eliminate wasted trips to laundry rooms.

Resource Optimization: Implementing a queue data structure for online booking to manage demand efficiently.

Security & Efficiency: Automatic email notifications to prompt timely laundry pickup, reducing theft risk.

02

Technical Integration

Hardware (IoT): M5Stack monitors using IMU sensors to detect machine vibration and movement patterns.

Middleware (Server): Node.js server acting as the "brain" to manage state, process sensor data, and handle queue management.

Client-Facing (Interface): Real-time front-end website with WebSocket integration for live updates and interactive dashboards.

03

UX/UI Features

User Pain Points Addressed: Eliminated "laundry anxiety" - the frustration of sitting and watching clothes spin or finding machines full after walking to the laundry room. Provided an "Instructions" feature to help students unfamiliar with machine settings, reducing damaged clothing incidents.

Interface Features: Real-time dashboards with progress bars, hierarchical information architecture (Building → Sub-building → Machine Type), and clear CTAs for "Join Queue" and "Notify Me".

User Feedback Loops: WebSocket integration for real-time UI updates and email notifications acting as push notifications to pull users back when necessary.

04

Key Metrics for Success

Established a clear technical threshold for "working" vs. "idle" machines based on accelerometric standard deviation. Machines with a cutoff value > 10 are classified as "working" (actively processing laundry), while values < 3 indicate "idle" status. This data-driven approach ensures accurate machine state detection and reliable user experience.
